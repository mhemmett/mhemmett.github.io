Seismic velocities decrease in the presence of open fluid-filled microfractures. We can model microcrack density as a second or fourth order tensor and map this to relative changes in seismic velocity, dv/v. Changes in dv/v over time would then signify temporal evolution of microcrack density, related to the stress field.

Peifeng Wang's dv/v study at Axial Seamount shows crack opening and closure, potentially a signal from magmatic recharge as well.

We have to consider anisotropic media with a preferential crack orientation, or rather two distinct orientations. dv/v is sensitive to the stiffness tensor $C_{ijkl}$.

Maochuan Zhang's focal mechanisms show the ratio of principal stress axes and their change over time

My work on shear-wave splitting gives sensitivity to the orientation of maximum compression and the relative strength of anisotropy, which is controlled by microcrack density.
