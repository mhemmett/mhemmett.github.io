Constraining Stress Evolution Across an Eruption Cycle at Axial Seamount Volcano

Plate tectonics drive many of the processes responsible for significant geohazards, from arc volcanism to massive subduction zone earthquakes (USGS, 2025). One key part of this cycle is seafloor spreading, where mantle magma upwells and erupts to form new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridge sites where plates diverge. This creation of new oceanic crust  – from mantle upwelling through magma storage and eruption – occurs through the eruption cycle of a mid-ocean ridge volcano (Tolstoy et al., 2006). One useful lens for understanding the dynamics of seafloor spreading includes constraining the geophysical stress field of a volcano and its evolution across an eruption cycle. It is critically important to constrain this process, but the complex geology and dynamics of a volcano, limitations in offshore instrumentation, and the issue of many non-linear, anisotropic components of the stress-strain relationship have thus far limited progress in this area of research. I will perform a joint stress inversion with three distinct studies on geophysical stress – focal mechanisms, shear-wave splitting, and relative seismic velocity variations – to computationally model and quantitatively constrain stress evolution across the Axial Seamount caldera over an entire eruption cycle.

Axial Seamount, the best instrumented submarine volcano in the world (Wilcock et al., 2018), is well-poised to constrain these critical processes. Many studies have probed the geology and structure of Axial Seamount, such that its dominant features controlling stress evolution can be accurately modeled (Chadwick and Nooner, 2016; Hefner et al., 2020; Slead et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2026; Kidiwela et al., in prep). Ongoing work at Axial Seamount is already constraining the geophysical stress field from these three different perspectives, though individually (Zhang et al., in prep; Wang et al., in prep). All three stress studies are built on a high quality machine learning-based earthquake catalog relocated with the double difference algorithm (Wang et al., 2024), lending further confidence to these measurements and the ability to interpret them jointly. 

P-wave focal mechanisms constrain stress orientation and relative contributions of components to the stress tensor (Massa et al., 2016). Shear-wave splitting parameters constrain the orientation of maximum horizontal compressional stress and changes in percent anisotropy across time (Silver and Chan, 1991; Kendall et al., 2025). This can be combined with a high quality S-wave velocity model, ray tracing and the splitting intensity measure (Chevrot, 2000) to model anisotropy in 4D. Ambient noise interferometry reveals relative changes in seismic wave propagation velocities (dv/v), which are related to anisotropy, pore pressure, and changes in the stress field (Donaldson et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2024). The primary challenge presented is the need for a unified theory of geophysical stress that integrates all three perspectives.

This study will develop a model for stress evolution at Axial Seamount volcano, integrated with a real-time monitoring framework for eruption prediction, while also advancing the fundamental understanding of geophysical stress and the seafloor spreading cycle. This framework for joint stress inversion will serve as an example for future monitoring on Cascade arc volcanoes that threaten major population centers.
