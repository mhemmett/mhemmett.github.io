For one split on one window, how is that single window determined? How is it done for Christian's version, for MFAST, and for SWSPy?

### Selecting a single window

**MFAST:** Performs hierarchical clustering over many windows. For one window, MFAST starts by picking a dataset-dependent initial window for first calculating SNR. Then the frequency analysis window is defined as 1.5 × the initial window. Then there is a spectral estimate over the frequency analysis window, computes a power spectrum, and picks the dominant frequency as the frequency of maximum spectral power. There are a couple of constraints on the dominant frequency to make sure it is at least `1 / snr_window_period` and at most `fs / 10`. So now we have a dominant frequency, then we calculate the dominant period as `Tmid = 1 / fd`.

Then `Tmid` is used to construct a series of windows, with the shortest equal to the length of `Tmid` and the longest equal to `2.5 × Tmid`. The number of window start and end times is set by the user. All of the windows, of varying lengths, can be calculated from this information. So the dominant period is used to set up the windows.

**SWSPy:** Performs density-based clustering over many windows.

**Baillard:** Looks at one single window. Defines one S-wave window based on the data, then computes spectral density to find dominant frequency in the initial window. Sets the window as `3 × T_dom`, so it gets multiple cycles and can produce a meaningful result. So it's quite different from MFAST in that the dominant period is directly used as the analysis window, where the dominant period is used to set up the lengths of windows that will be considered in MFAST.

### Comparing splitting results, SWSPy vs. Baillard

SWSPy returns a result with a delay time that is consistent with Baillard's results, but the actual polarization angle / fast direction is quite different. This is quite confusing. It's possible that the clustering results returned a more robust direction, but the delay time was comparable. It's difficult to compare SWSPy vs. Baillard 1:1 by plots because the SWSPy splitting is done in LQT space rather than ZNE, but the output direction is φ degrees from North. It's possible that Baillard and SWSPy are defining fast direction by different conventions — I will check.

Here's the discrepancy:
- **Baillard** defines fast direction by trigonometric convention, as **CCW from East**.
- **SWSPy** defines fast direction as **degrees CW from North**.

Conversion factor (in degrees): `φ_{CCW from E} = − (φ_{CW from N} + 90)`, then convert to radians with `× π / 180`.

We find that there is still some discrepancy between SWSPy and Baillard after conversion, but it's relatively small. Same with the discrepancy in delay time. This makes sense, as the global minima are often a region rather than one particular spot.

MFAST uses the convention of **CW from North** as well, rather than CCW from East. This is consistent with Silver and Chan (1991), and the azimuth convention in seismology.

Thus, we see that SWSPy returns results relatively consistent with Baillard's method, and it already integrates a method of windowing and clustering. **Since this is a published methodology that already integrates clustering and is fully built in Python, we should just use SWSPy for the shear-wave splitting.**

## Literature on MFAST and SWSPy

### MFAST
- Mengesha et al. (2024), "Time-Varying Crustal Anisotropy at Whakaari/White Island Volcano." Applies SWS with MFAST at the most active volcano in New Zealand, 2-year earthquake catalog 2018–2020. Compare splitting results to Vp/Vs ratios spatially and temporally and conclude that the observed anisotropy is truly due to stress reorientation from moving magma. <https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2023GL106473>
- Okada, Savage et al. (2024), "Shear-wave splitting and seismic velocity structure in the focal area of the earthquake swarm and their relation with earthquake-swarm activity in the Noto Peninsula, central Japan." Investigates an earthquake swarm with seismic velocity tomography and SWS. As the swarm migrates, they identify stress-induced anisotropy in the south that switches to fault-structure anisotropy toward the north. They use MFAST. <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40623-024-01974-0>
- Bacon et al. (2021), "On the Origin of Seismic Anisotropy in the Shallow Crust of the Northern Volcanic Zone, Iceland." MFAST SWS around this volcano. Observed anisotropy is consistent with Mid-Atlantic Ridge divergence. Highest anisotropy in the areas with the most earthquakes, consistent with brittle deformation. <https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2021JB022655>

### SWSPy
- Hudson et al. (2023), "Automated shear-wave splitting analysis for single- and multi-layer anisotropic media." The SWSPy paper, applied to a few case studies. <https://seismica.library.mcgill.ca/article/view/1031>
- Hudson et al. (2024), "Seismic Node Arrays for Enhanced Understanding and Monitoring of Geothermal Systems." Uses a seismic node array deployed at a geothermal site in England. Looks at anisotropy from above using SWS and corrects splitting to approximate S-wave source polarizations. Compares to P-wave focal mechanisms to see distinction between stress orientation of induced seismicity vs. broader regional stress direction. <https://pubs.geoscienceworld.org/ssa/tsr/article/4/3/161/645701>
- Hudson et al. (2023), "Hydrothermal Fluids and Where to Find Them: Using Seismic Attenuation and Anisotropy to Map Fluids Beneath Uturuncu Volcano, Bolivia." Combines P- and S-wave attenuation with SWSPy SWS and conductivity/resistivity studies to (1) map fluids, (2) constrain their chemical composition (e.g., heavy-metal brines relevant for potential geothermal sources / resource extraction). <https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2022GL100974>

### Other
- Wolf et al. (2025), "Comprehensive global dataset of uniformly processed shear-wave splitting measurements." Global mantle anisotropy using SplitRacerAuto on global earthquakes ≥ 5.9 since 2004. ~90k good measurements of splitting and ~210k "null" measurements (mantle below that station is effectively isotropic w.r.t. anisotropy). <https://www.semanticscholar.org/reader/52e23df0a3cbb61709483dc236b17db56aa66952>
- Other papers use SplitLab or SplitPy.
